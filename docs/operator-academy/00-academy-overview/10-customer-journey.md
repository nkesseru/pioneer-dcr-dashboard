# 10 — The Customer Journey

> Required by the [Constitution](../CONSTITUTION.md), Pillar 3 — First Value Obsession.
> Every operator must be able to name where every account sits on this map.

Customers don't churn at random. They drop off at a specific stage. Operators who can name the stage can fix the drop.

## The Ten Stages

```
   Lead
    │
    ▼
   Sale
    │
    ▼
   Onboarding
    │
    ▼
   First Value          ◄── single biggest leverage point
    │
    ▼
   Habit
    │
    ▼
   Adoption
    │
    ▼
   Business Outcome     ◄── the renewal is decided here
    │
    ▼
   Renewal
    │
    ▼
   Expansion
    │
    ▼
   Advocacy
```

A customer can move backwards. A great account that loses its champion can fall from Adoption back to Habit overnight. Operators watch the map for backward motion.

---

## Stage-by-stage: what good looks like

For each stage, three questions:

1. **When does the customer enter?**
2. **When does the customer exit?**
3. **What does the operator do here?**

---

### Stage 1 — Lead

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | A prospect shows interest. Inbound form, referral, sales outreach. |
| **Exits when** | They sign a contract. |
| **Operator role** | Light. Watch the sales conversation if the deal is strategic. Take notes for handoff. |

The operator does not own this stage. But the handoff at the end of Stage 1 determines whether Stage 2 goes well.

---

### Stage 2 — Sale

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Contract signed. |
| **Exits when** | The kickoff call is scheduled. |
| **Operator role** | Read the contract. Read every word of the sales handoff. Schedule the kickoff. |

The shortest stage. If the kickoff isn't scheduled within 3 business days of signing, that's a signal: the customer's excitement is already fading.

---

### Stage 3 — Onboarding

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Kickoff call held. |
| **Exits when** | First Value (Stage 4) is reached. |
| **Operator role** | Drive setup. Import data. Train field techs + admin. Stay close every day. |

This is the operator's most hands-on stage. Most customer churn that happens in year one is set up by sloppy onboarding.

**Target time in stage:** 14 days.

---

### Stage 4 — First Value

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Customer experiences a real outcome. Not "logged in." A real result. |
| **Exits when** | They start coming back without prompting. |
| **Operator role** | Make sure the outcome is real, named, and memorable. |

The single biggest leverage point in the customer's life with Pioneer.

**What "first value" looks like for a Pioneer customer:**

- The owner pulls Mission Control and catches a $4,200 labor leak
- The admin sees a real-time inspection score for a customer site
- The CEO sees their first complete weekly Customer Economics view

If the operator cannot name the First Value moment for an account, the customer probably hasn't had one. Fix that this week.

---

### Stage 5 — Habit

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Customer starts using PioneerOps without prompting. |
| **Exits when** | They use it as part of a real decision (Adoption). |
| **Operator role** | Build the habit on purpose. Don't assume it forms. |

**Operator moves at Habit:**

- Set up weekly review rhythm (with the customer's CEO, not just admin)
- Send the Monday-morning Mission Control summary
- Anchor the product to a recurring decision (payroll Mondays, QBR quarterly)

If the customer falls out of habit, they fall back to "we'll get to it later" — and "later" is when churn lives.

---

### Stage 6 — Adoption

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Customer makes a real decision using PioneerOps data. |
| **Exits when** | The decision produces a real business outcome (Stage 7). |
| **Operator role** | Document the decision. Quantify it. Use it in the QBR. |

Adoption is where the product stops being software and starts being part of how they run the business.

**Examples of adoption decisions:**

- "We're not renewing the contract with vendor X — Customer Economics shows we're losing money on them."
- "We're raising rates 8% on these six customers next quarter — RPLH says it's overdue."
- "We're sending Mike to that site instead of Joe — DCR quality scores justify it."

---

### Stage 7 — Business Outcome

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | A decision they made using Pioneer produces a measurable result. |
| **Exits when** | The renewal conversation begins. |
| **Operator role** | Capture the outcome. Tell the story back to the customer. Make it undeniable. |

The renewal is decided in this stage, not at renewal time.

**Operator moves at Business Outcome:**

- Quantify the result ("This decision saved you $14,200/month.")
- Tell the story back to the customer in the QBR
- Make the outcome visible to anyone in the customer's company who matters

Customers don't renew because they like the product. They renew because they can point to results.

---

### Stage 8 — Renewal

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | 90 days before contract end. |
| **Exits when** | Renewal signed (or churn declared). |
| **Operator role** | Run the renewal motion. Surface risk early. Don't wait for the email. |

If the operator did Stages 1–7 well, Renewal is a 30-minute conversation. If they did them badly, Renewal is a save play under pressure.

---

### Stage 9 — Expansion

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Customer is renewing AND has unmet needs Pioneer can serve. |
| **Exits when** | The expansion contract is signed. |
| **Operator role** | Surface the expansion. Build the case. Hand it to the customer like an obvious next step. |

**Three standard expansion plays** (covered in [Pillar 06](../06-revenue-operations/README.md)):

1. Multi-location — second location, third location
2. Premium tier — they've outgrown the base tier
3. Add-on module — specific operational pain we can solve

Never pitch expansion before the basics work. If the customer is shaky at Stage 6, do not pitch Stage 9.

---

### Stage 10 — Advocacy

| | |
|---|---|
| **Enters when** | Customer recommends Pioneer to others without being asked. |
| **Exits when** | Never (advocacy compounds). |
| **Operator role** | Make it easy. Refer business back. Treat them better than new customers. |

Advocates are Pioneer's growth engine.

**Operator moves at Advocacy:**

- Bring them into product feedback conversations (they earn it)
- Offer them first look at new modules
- Make them feel like an owner, not a customer

---

## How to use the map

### In Monday pipeline reviews

For every owned account, the operator names the current stage. Out loud. Out loud forces honesty.

```
"ACME Cleaning is at Habit. Last week they were at First Value.
The next gate is Adoption. My plan this week is X."
```

If the operator can't name the stage, they don't know the account.

### In QBRs

Open the QBR by showing the customer where they are on the journey. CEOs love this. It tells them the operator has a plan, not just a status report.

### In churn-risk diagnosis

When health drops, the first question is "what stage are they at, and have they fallen backwards?" Most churns trace to a backward move.

### In onboarding

The whole purpose of the first 30 days is to move the customer from Sale → Onboarding → First Value. If the operator is doing other things during this window, they are doing the wrong things.

## The map at a glance

| # | Stage | Days from sign (target) | Operator focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Lead | — | Watch handoff |
| 2 | Sale | day 0 | Schedule kickoff |
| 3 | Onboarding | 0–14 | Drive setup |
| 4 | First Value | 14 | Make outcome real |
| 5 | Habit | 14–60 | Build routine |
| 6 | Adoption | 60–120 | Drive decisions |
| 7 | Business Outcome | 90–180 | Capture results |
| 8 | Renewal | day 275–365 | Run the motion |
| 9 | Expansion | renewal time | Surface obvious next step |
| 10 | Advocacy | year 2+ | Make them feel like owners |

## The Pioneer Test on this framework

- ✅ Connects activity to outcome at every stage
- ✅ Plain English, no MBA jargon
- ✅ Real customer examples
- ✅ Operators can actually use it Monday morning
- ✅ Nick would talk about customers this way

Pass.

---

## What this changes for the curriculum

Every lesson in every pillar must reference the Customer Journey. The lesson's worked example should explicitly call out:

> "This skill matters most at Stage X. Without it, customers stall here and never reach Stage Y."

If a lesson cannot connect to the journey, it is teaching software, not business improvement. Rewrite.
