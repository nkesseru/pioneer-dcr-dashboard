# 07 — Executive Advisor

Talking to commercial cleaning company owners and CEOs as a peer, not a vendor.

Maps to **Pillar 07** in the [Competency Model](../00-academy-overview/02-competency-model.md).

This is the highest-stakes pillar. It's also the hardest to fake. Customer CEOs read fakers in 30 seconds.

## The core stance

The operator is **not** the customer's vendor in the executive conversation. They're a thinking partner who happens to know the customer's tools cold AND the customer's industry well AND has the data the customer doesn't have time to pull themselves.

That stance is earned by:

- Knowing the customer's business as deeply as they do (Pillar 02)
- Knowing the platform that runs it (Pillar 03)
- Knowing the broader industry context (Pillar 07.2)
- Bringing real data to every conversation (Pillar 04)
- Holding a position when it matters (Pillar 07.4)

If you can do all five, you're an advisor. If you skip any, you're a vendor.

## Mandatory reading rotation

Every operator subscribes to at least 3 commercial cleaning / facilities industry publications. They read at least one piece per week. They share one insight to the team per month.

Suggested sources:

- BSCAI member content
- ISSA Today
- CleanLink
- Facility Cleaning Decisions
- One general business / labor-economics source (WSJ, Bloomberg, etc.) for context

## Mandatory artifact

The Executive Briefing exercise (see [graduation standards](../00-academy-overview/07-graduation-standards.md)) — a 15-minute briefing to a CEO-level audience using a strategic frame, real data, and three recommendations. Recorded.

This is the single hardest artifact in the graduation portfolio. Operators should rehearse it 5–10 times before live delivery.

## Patterns the operator must know cold

### "Most cleaning company CEOs care about three things"

1. Can I make payroll
2. Are my customers happy enough to stay
3. Is my margin going up or down

Every executive conversation should connect to at least one of these. If your topic doesn't connect, you're talking about features, not outcomes.

### "Translate every number into either time or money"

- "Your RPLH is $9 below target" → "On your current volume, that's about $14,000/month in left-on-the-table revenue"
- "Health score 60" → "Two customers are at real risk of leaving in the next 90 days"
- "AR > 60 days is 12%" → "About $40,000 you've earned but haven't been paid for"

CEOs translate stats into time or money in their heads anyway. Save them the step.

### "Bring three recommendations, prioritized, with effort"

A QBR with one recommendation feels thin. A QBR with eight feels overwhelming. Three is the sweet spot, and they should be ranked.

Each recommendation has:

- One sentence of WHAT
- One sentence of WHY (the data behind it)
- One number of EFFORT (hours, weeks, dollars)
- One number of IMPACT (revenue or saved cost)

If you can't fill those four for a recommendation, it isn't ready.
