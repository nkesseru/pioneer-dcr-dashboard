# Executive Briefing Exercise

A 15-minute strategic briefing delivered by the operator to an internal panel playing the role of a customer CEO + senior team. The capstone artifact for Operator II certification.

This is the artifact that most often catches under-cooked operators. It also tells you the most about whether someone is ready to talk to customer CEOs unsupervised.

## Operator prep — done before the session

The operator picks one of the briefing topics (see below) and prepares:

- A 5–8 slide deck (or doc — operator's choice)
- A 1-page executive summary handed out at the start
- Numbers verified twice
- Three recommendations with ROI math
- 2–3 anticipated hostile questions and answers
- Rehearsed at least 3 times (alone or with peers)

The operator should NOT rehearse with the trainer. The trainer is on the panel; rehearsal with them defeats the exercise.

---

## Topic options

The operator picks one. Each is a real strategic conversation a commercial cleaning company CEO needs to have.

### Topic A — Revenue Per Labor Hour over 12 months

"Here's how to think about your Revenue Per Labor Hour over the next 12 months."

Required elements:

- Current company RPLH (from Customer Economics)
- Target RPLH and gap
- Two structural causes of the gap
- Three recommendations with ROI math
- 12-month projection if recommendations land

### Topic B — Operational changes PioneerOps unlocks

"Three structural changes to your operation that PioneerOps unlocks."

Required elements:

- What the customer's current state looks like
- The three changes (specific, prioritized)
- ROI math for each
- Sequencing — which one first, which second, why

### Topic C — Customer concentration risk

"Your customer concentration risk and how to dilute it."

Required elements:

- Current top-5 customer concentration
- Why this is a risk (not a stability story)
- Three dilution strategies
- Timeline + costs

### Topic D — AR aging and what it reveals about sales

"Why your current AR aging tells me something about your sales process."

Required elements:

- Current AR aging breakdown
- Pattern: which customers age, and what they have in common
- The upstream sales-process implication
- Three changes to address it

### Topic E — Labor market shift implications

"The labor market is shifting; here's what it means for your business."

Required elements:

- Macro labor context (wages, availability, regulation)
- Local-market specifics
- How PioneerOps data supports adaptation
- Three responses, sequenced

## During the briefing

### What the panel does

1. **Sit in role.** Don't be friendly. Don't be unrealistically hostile either. Be the customer CEO you've seen most often — direct, busy, skeptical of features, hungry for value.

2. **Time-keep.** The operator gets 15 minutes. If they hit 14:30, call it. If they hit 15:30, stop them. Real CEOs don't extend the meeting.

3. **Ask the hard questions in Q&A.** Pre-write 5 hostile-but-fair questions. Pick 2–3 to ask. Examples:

   - "Your three recommendations cost me $X. What's my ROI for each, in dollars, in 12 months?"
   - "How do I know your numbers are right? My CFO will check."
   - "If this is so important, why isn't every PioneerOps customer doing it?"
   - "What happens to this plan if my best route manager leaves in the next 90 days?"
   - "I've heard versions of this from three other vendors. What's actually different about yours?"

4. **Take notes.** Score against the rubric in real-time so you don't backfill from memory.

### What the operator does

- Open with the executive summary (1 minute max)
- Land the briefing in 12–13 minutes, leaving room for transitions
- Use numbers from PioneerOps, industry data, or real customer benchmarks (not made up)
- Handle Q&A without folding to pushback OR getting defensive
- Close with a clear next step ("I'd like 30 minutes next week to walk through implementation")

## Rubric

Each dimension scored 0–4.

| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic framing (not feature pitch) | | |
| Real numbers, traceable | | |
| Three recommendations with ROI math | | |
| Narrative arc (one story, not 12) | | |
| Time discipline (15 min, not 17) | | |
| Pushback handling (held position) | | |
| CEO-appropriate language (no jargon) | | |
| Close (concrete next step) | | |

### Bar for Operator II certification

- All dimensions ≥ 3
- At least 2 dimensions at 4
- No dimension at 0 or 1

### Bar for Senior Operator certification

- All dimensions at 4
- Panel verdict: "I would book a real meeting with this person if I were a cleaning company CEO"

## Debrief structure (15 min)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:05 | Operator self-rates each dimension and explains why |
| 0:05–0:10 | Panel feedback (one keep + one change per panelist) |
| 0:10–0:13 | Replay one inflection point with a different approach |
| 0:13–0:15 | Operator commits to one specific change before next briefing |

The debrief is more valuable than the briefing itself. Don't skip it.

---

## Common failure modes

- ❌ **Opens with a thank-you.** Real CEOs don't have time for niceties. Open with the punch line.
- ❌ **Feature talk.** "PioneerOps lets you..." is wrong framing. "Your operation needs to..." is right.
- ❌ **No real numbers.** Generic claims ("efficiency gains") don't survive Q&A.
- ❌ **Three recommendations of the same shape.** "Do A, B, and C" — when A, B, and C are all software configs — isn't a strategic briefing.
- ❌ **Folds on pushback.** The CEO disagrees with rec #2. Operator immediately concedes. Bad sign.
- ❌ **Overruns the time.** "I just need 30 more seconds" multiple times = the operator hasn't done the work.
- ❌ **Closes without a next step.** "Let me know what you think" is not a close.

## What a great briefing looks like

| Marker | What you'd hear |
|---|---|
| Open | "Three things I want you to walk out with today. Then I'll spend 12 minutes on the data behind them." |
| Frame | "Your company is going to get 4–7% gross margin compression over the next 18 months. Here's what PioneerOps data tells me about how to defend." |
| Numbers | "Your RPLH is $58.40 against my $62 target. That gap, against your hours, equals about $14,200 per month." |
| Rec | "Reprice these 6 customers by 8% on renewal. Customer-specific argument for each. We can have those conversations by November." |
| Q&A | (CEO pushes back) "I understand the concern. Here's the math on the downside case. Even if 2 of the 6 churn — which I don't expect — net revenue is up 6%." |
| Close | "I'll send you the customer-by-customer detail by Friday. I'd like 30 minutes next Wednesday to talk through the first three conversations together." |

That's what we're training operators to deliver.

## After the exercise

The recording, the deck, and the rubric scores go into the operator's graduation portfolio. They're also added to the Academy archive — future operators study them.

If the operator passes: Operator II certification (assuming other artifacts pass).

If the operator partial-passes: 30 days to re-deliver with specific gaps closed.

If the operator fails: trainer + operator review whether to extend training, change roles, or end the engagement.
