## ELEMENT 1 — Plain-English Explanation

### What a QBR actually is

A QBR — Quarterly Business Review — is a **30-minute conversation with a customer CEO where the Operator turns a quarter's worth of operational truth into three priced executive decisions.**

Read that sentence twice. Every word is doing work:

| Word | What it really means |
|---|---|
| **30-minute** | Not 60. Not 90. The discipline of compression is part of the work. |
| **Conversation** | Not a presentation. The CEO talks at least 30% of the time. |
| **Customer CEO** | The owner or top decision-maker. Not their assistant, not their bookkeeper. |
| **Operator turns** | The Operator brings the synthesis. The CEO does not assemble the picture themselves. |
| **Operational truth** | What actually happened (Pillar 8 — the work, recorded). |
| **Three** | Not seven. Not two. The discipline of forced prioritization. |
| **Priced** | Every recommendation carries the cost of inaction in dollars (Pillar 7). |
| **Executive decisions** | The CEO leaves with three things they will decide on, by named dates. |

### What a QBR is NOT

| Confusion | Reality |
|---|---|
| QBR = status report | Status is what the CEO reads in email. QBR is a decision-forcing function. |
| QBR = celebrate the wins | Wins are a credibility deposit, not the agenda. |
| QBR = present the dashboards | The CEO can see the dashboards. They need synthesis, not screen-shares. |
| QBR = relationship maintenance | A QBR that produces no decision was a relationship event, not a QBR. |
| QBR = pitch the next module | QBR is for THIS quarter's loop closure. Pitch lives in a separate conversation. |

### The simple math behind why QBRs matter

A trained Operator delivers ~12 QBRs per quarter for an Operator II portfolio (8–12 customers, every customer + some extras). Across a year, that's 48 QBRs.

- Untrained CSM: ~10% of QBRs produce an executive decision. ~5 decisions/year.
- Trained Operator: 70%+ of QBRs produce an executive decision. ~34 decisions/year.

**The difference between 5 and 34 customer-CEO-approved decisions per year is the entire Operator role.**

## ELEMENT 2 — Why It Matters

### The renewal is decided here, not at renewal time

Most cleaning companies renew their software vendor on the strength of the last conversation they had with the operator. The last conversation was, by definition, the most recent QBR.

A QBR that converted into priced decisions and verified outcomes makes the renewal feel obvious. A QBR that was 40 slides of activity makes the renewal feel like a tax. The renewal price tag does not change — the renewal *energy* does.

### The QBR is the Operator's compounding asset

Every QBR is a deposit or withdrawal on the Operator's **credibility account** with that CEO:

| Move | Effect on the account |
|---|---|
| Predicted X. Verified X. Showed the math. | Big deposit. |
| Predicted Y. Verified Y didn't happen. Said so first. Diagnosed why. | Big deposit. (Yes — bigger than the win.) |
| Predicted Z. Worked. Did not document it. | Small deposit. (Credit unclaimed.) |
| Predicted W. Failed. Didn't bring it up. CEO discovered later. | Massive withdrawal. Often unrecoverable. |
| Showed up with no predictions, just a status update. | No deposit, no withdrawal. Account stagnates. |

**Credibility decay rule:** an Operator's credibility decays one QBR at a time when they stop carrying predictions and verifications. Most CSMs lose this account silently across a year and only realize when renewal goes sideways.

### CEOs pay for outcomes; they tolerate features

A CEO who paid $X/month for 12 months expects to see $Y in business outcomes they can name. If they cannot, the math does not work for them, no matter how much they like the operator.

The QBR is where the Operator makes the math work — by naming the outcomes, pricing them, attributing them to the loop, and proposing the next three.

### The QBR is the only place an Operator's full doctrine fires at once

| Doctrine | How it shows up in a QBR |
|---|---|
| **Pillar 1 — Outcomes, not features** | The opening sentence names outcomes, not screens |
| **Pillar 2 — Diagnostician** | Every observation is followed by a cause, not a complaint |
| **Pillar 3 — First Value / Journey** | The CEO sees where they sit on the journey, what the next gate is |
| **Pillar 4 — Scaling Through Systems** | The QBR is a SYSTEM (spine, prep discipline, follow-up artifact, verification dates) — not a performance |
| **Pillar 5 — AI division of labor** | AI prepared the prep; the Operator carries the judgment and the signature |
| **Pillar 7 — Economic Reality** | Every finding has a price; every recommendation has an inaction cost |
| **Pillar 8 — Work Becomes the Record** | Every claim points back to a record the CEO can audit |
| **Thesis 16 — Executive Communication** | Lead with the conclusion. 90-second rule. Three recommendations. Hallway-passable. |

If a QBR doesn't fire all eight, it isn't a QBR — it's a meeting.

### The QBR as a System (Pillar 4)

**The QBR is not a charismatic moment. It is a system.**

A system that produces decisions on schedule. A system that compounds credibility across quarters. A system that does not depend on any one Operator's charm or the CEO's mood.

The same Operator delivering the same spine with the same prep discipline will produce roughly the same approval rate across customers. That is the test of Pillar 4. If your QBR results vary wildly by your mood, the day of the week, or whether the CEO had coffee — you have not yet built a system. You have a performance.

Performances do not scale. Systems do.

**What makes the QBR a system:**

- The 30-minute spine (fixed structure across customers)
- The prep discipline (the 8-minute prep test, the named hostile scenario)
- The artifact (the 6-slide or 2-page doc, the 24-hour follow-up)
- The verification dates (machinery, not memory)
- The loop closure (every QBR closes the loop from the prior one)
- The replicable rubric (the scoring criteria for Q10 are the operator's self-test)

A new Operator inheriting an existing account should be able to deliver a credible QBR within 60 days of the handoff — because the system is documented. If they can't, the prior Operator built a performance instead of a system. That's a coaching gap, not an organizational mystery.

## ELEMENT 3 — Pioneer Example

### The 11-minute conversation that re-priced six customers

Pioneer customer "Riverside Office Park" was Pioneer's third-largest customer by revenue. Their owner had been quietly grumbling about Pioneer's monthly fee for two quarters. April delivered the Q3 QBR knowing she was walking into a hostile room.

**What April did NOT do:**

- Lead with thanks for the partnership
- Open with usage stats
- Walk through Mission Control screens
- Defend the price

**What April did:**

**Minute 0–1 (opening):**

> "Three decisions by the end of this conversation. First — you're losing about $14,200 a month on six customers you haven't repriced in three years. Second — your cash conversion has stretched 9 days in 6 months, which is $48,000 in cash you're not currently holding. Third — your inspector coverage is at 78%, and 22% of your buildings cannot be defended if you're audited this year. Now let me show you the math behind each one. We have 25 minutes."

**Minute 1–9 (state of the business):**

April walked the four vitals using one screen pulled from Customer Economics — not a deck. Each vital got 2 minutes, a trend chart, and a sentence ending in dollars.

**Minute 9–17 (loop closure on last quarter):**

> "Last quarter we predicted three things: (1) tighten your supply ordering would save $4,300/month — verified, $4,650/month. (2) Reassign Mike to Plant Floor 2 would lift inspection scores 8% — verified, 11%. (3) The rate increase letter to Acme Logistics would land within 30 days at 6% — failed, the customer pushed back, we got 4% after 45 days. Net $9,800/month in verified gain plus a smaller-than-planned rate increase."

The owner had not expected to hear the failure named first.

**Minute 17–27 (the three recommendations):**

Each rec arrived in the same structure:

- One sentence of WHAT (specific action)
- One sentence of WHY (cause, traced to a metric)
- One number for COST OF INACTION (dollars per month)
- One number for EFFORT (dollars or hours to act)
- One DATE for verification (when we measure if it worked)

April's three recs that day:

1. Re-price the six customers we identified — cost of inaction $14,200/month; effort: April drafts six letters, owner approves, sends within 14 days; verification: 60 days from send
2. Address cash conversion by changing the closeout workflow — cost of inaction roughly $5,300/month in stretched AR; effort: one-day workflow change; verification: 90 days
3. Cover the audit-exposure gap — 12 buildings need inspections within 30 days, no new module needed; effort: two additional inspector days/month, ~$1,400/month; cost of inaction: indefensible compliance position worth a contract

**Minute 27–28 (close):**

> "I need your decision on these three by Friday. The math is in the doc I just shared with you. Want me to draft the customer letters in the meantime so you're ready to go on rec one?"

**Minute 28–30 (CEO talked):**

The owner — who had walked in planning to negotiate Pioneer's price down — agreed to all three. He also paid for a workshop the following month for his own ops team to learn how to read Customer Economics.

The conversation never went near Pioneer's monthly cost.

### Nick's view

> "April's QBR didn't 'save' the account. She prevented a salvage operation from being needed. The owner came in to negotiate. He left having made business decisions. The renewal three months later was a 60-second conversation."

## ELEMENT 4 — Customer Example

### How a QBR saved a renewal worth $186,000

**The setup.** "Northwest Janitorial Services" — Pioneer customer for 14 months. Owner: Mark Sutter, second-generation. Mid-size cleaning company, ~$2.4M revenue.

Two weeks before the QBR, Mark received an unsolicited proposal from a competitor at 22% less per month. He came to the QBR planning to give 30 days notice.

The Operator (call him Jay) found out about the competitor proposal three days before the QBR — from one of Mark's route managers, not from Mark. **This is field reality leading the conversation, not the customer's stated agenda.**

### What Jay prepared

Jay knew the price gap was indefensible by itself. Northwest paid Pioneer $14,500/month; the competitor's offer was $11,300/month. That is a real difference.

Jay did the math:

- Pioneer's Customer Economics had surfaced $9,800/month in Northwest's portfolio savings over the last 9 months (verified)
- Pioneer's inspection module had identified — and caused Northwest to fix — three recurring quality issues at the company's two healthcare accounts
- Pioneer's records showed Northwest could defend every invoice from the last 9 months with documented work; the competitor's product had no equivalent

The price gap was $3,200/month. Pioneer's documented value was $9,800/month plus an unpriced compliance defense. The math was on Pioneer's side; Jay had to make Mark see it.

### The QBR

**Opening:**

> "Mark, three decisions by the end. First — we've delivered $88,000 in verified savings this year, and I'll walk you through the receipts. Second — we have a $3,200/month price gap with a competitor who can't show you what's about to be on the table. Third — there are two open recommendations on the table that could deliver another $42,000 next year if you approve them today. Twenty-eight minutes."

Mark, who had planned to give notice in the first three minutes, sat back.

**Minutes 1–9 — state of the business:**

Jay walked the four vitals. The Operational Exposure vital is what landed.

> "If the state of Washington's healthcare compliance audit drops on you next quarter — and they're auditing your two largest healthcare accounts on a rotating schedule, so it's a real possibility — you can produce 248 inspection records covering 100% of those buildings. Each record has the inspector's name, the date, the building scope walked, the issues found, and the resolution. The competitor cannot show you that capability because they do not have it."

Mark: "How do you know they don't have it?"

Jay: "I pulled their product documentation last week. Their platform does scheduling and time. It does not do scored inspections or defensible compliance records. Switching means losing 14 months of compliance history and starting your record from zero on day one."

Mark went quiet.

**Minutes 9–17 — loop closure:**

Jay walked through every prediction from the prior three QBRs. Including the two that failed.

> "Q1 — we predicted Tristar would accept a 7% rate increase. They accepted 5%. We undershot. Q2 — we predicted the new closeout workflow would shorten DSO by 8 days. It shortened by 11. We overshot. Q3 — we predicted Mike's reassignment to Greenway Office Plaza would lift inspection scores 8%. It lifted 14%. We overshot. Three predictions, two undershoots, one undershoot. Net $88,000 in verified savings."

Mark had not realized the number was that big. Jay had it documented to the dollar because the loop closure had been recorded every quarter (Pillar 8).

**Minutes 17–27 — the three recommendations:**

The third one — covering the audit-exposure gap on two more accounts — got Mark to do the math himself out loud. By minute 25, Mark was asking which one he should do FIRST.

**Minutes 27–30 — close:**

Mark: "What do I do about the competitor?"

Jay (the operator): "That is your call. I'd tell them you're staying because Pioneer can prove your work and they can't, and your compliance position is worth more than $3,200 a month."

Mark renewed for 18 months and added a third location.

### The result

| Outcome | Value |
|---|---|
| Renewal (12 months) | $174,000 |
| Third location added (12 months) | $36,000 first year |
| Two additional recommendations executed | $42,000 portfolio savings |
| **Total Q4 value** | **$252,000** |

The competitor never heard from Mark again.

### What this customer example teaches

- **Field reality leads.** Jay knew about the competitor offer because he was close enough to the field that a route manager mentioned it. Without that signal, he would have walked into the QBR cold.
- **Pillar 8 wins renewals.** The 248 inspection records were the unbeatable card. The work had become the record; the record had become the truth; the truth was the renewal.
- **Loop closure is the credibility deposit.** Mark agreed to the three recommendations because the operator had been right about the things he'd been right about AND honest about the things he'd been wrong about.
- **The competitor's price was real.** The Operator did not pretend the gap didn't exist. He named it, priced the gap against documented value, and let the math win.

## ELEMENT 5 — Common Mistakes

The QBR is the single most common place Operators fail in the field. The mistakes are predictable. Recognize them in yourself before you make them on stage.

### Mistake 1 — The Status Update QBR

> **Sounds like:** "Here's what we did in Q3. We onboarded three new buildings, completed 89 inspections, updated your assignment matrix three times, and resolved 14 support requests…"
>
> **Why it fails:** The CEO didn't want a status update. They wanted a decision-forcing function. After 8 minutes of activity recap, the CEO has lost the capacity to make a meaningful decision.
>
> **Upgrade:** "Here are three decisions you'll make today. Then I'll spend the rest of the time earning the right to recommend them."

### Mistake 2 — The Naked Problem

> **Sounds like:** "Your inspection coverage has dropped to 73%. We thought you should know."
>
> **Why it fails:** A problem without a price tag, without a recommendation, and without a verification date is delegation upward. You just made the CEO do your job.
>
> **Upgrade:** "Your inspection coverage has dropped to 73%, which means 14 buildings are out of compliance protection. That's roughly $X/month in exposure. I recommend two changes to inspector scheduling that would close it within 30 days. Verification on March 1."

### Mistake 3 — The Data Dump

> **Sounds like:** "Here's your scorecard for the quarter. Fourteen metrics. Let me walk through them one by one…"
>
> **Why it fails:** CEOs are not paid to absorb 14 metrics. They are paid to make 1–3 decisions. Volume is the enemy of decision.
>
> **Upgrade:** "Three vitals moved this quarter — two in your favor, one against. Two minutes on each. Then three recommendations."

### Mistake 4 — The Feature Talk QBR

> **Sounds like:** "We released the new Mission Control card this quarter — let me show you the new view. Also the inspection module has new scoring options…"
>
> **Why it fails:** Feature talk converts an outcome conversation into a product demo. The CEO did not buy product. They bought outcomes. They tolerate product talk only when nothing else is going well.
>
> **Upgrade:** Mention features ONLY as the means to an outcome. "We've added a view that surfaces customers below your RPLH target faster. Here are the three customers that view caught this quarter that we previously would have missed for another 30 days."

### Mistake 5 — The Defensive QBR

> **Sounds like:** "We failed to hit the prediction from last quarter because the customer pushed back. We tried our best…"
>
> **Why it fails:** Defensiveness is a credibility withdrawal. CEOs trust people who own their misses, not people who explain them.
>
> **Upgrade:** "Last quarter we predicted a 7% rate increase. We landed 4%. The customer's CFO pushed back on the comparable rates. I should have predicted the pushback. Here's what I learned, and here's how I'll do the next one differently."

### Mistake 6 — The No-Verification QBR

> **Sounds like:** "Let's set up some action items for the quarter. We'll talk again in three months."
>
> **Why it fails:** "Action items" without a pre-declared expected result and a verification date are wishes, not commitments. They evaporate the moment the meeting ends.
>
> **Upgrade:** "Recommendation 1: change the supply ordering workflow. Expected result: $4,300/month in supply savings. Verification: 60 days from change. If we hit at least $3,000/month, we keep the change. If we don't, we reverse it."

### Mistake 7 — The 60-Slide QBR

> **Sounds like:** A slide deck with 60 slides, including 12 of methodology, 18 of historical context, and 24 of detailed dashboards.
>
> **Why it fails:** Compression is comprehension's proof. Sixty slides means the Operator hasn't decided what matters. The CEO won't either.
>
> **Upgrade:** Six slides maximum. State of the business (1). Loop closure (1). Recommendation 1 (1). Recommendation 2 (1). Recommendation 3 (1). Close (1). Everything else is appendix.

### Mistake 8 — The Pitch Inside the QBR

> **Sounds like:** "Now let me show you our new analytics tier — only $X/month additional…"
>
> **Why it fails:** A QBR is not a sales call. The moment you pitch, the CEO downgrades your credibility account from "advisor" to "vendor."
>
> **Upgrade:** Pitch in a separate conversation, scheduled for a separate week. Let the QBR be clean.

### Mistake 9 — The QBR That Fails Because the Record Can't Support the Claim (Pillar 8)

This is the doctrine-grade failure mode. The lesson where Pillar 8 becomes felt instead of stated.

> **The story.** Sara, a Pioneer operator, delivered a Q2 QBR to a mid-size cleaning company. Her headline finding: "Your labor efficiency improved 12% this quarter."
>
> The CEO sat forward. "Twelve percent? Show me where."
>
> Sara had built the 12% number from a Mission Control summary. The number was correct in aggregate. But the underlying records lived in three different systems and her synthesis had used a sampling approach. When the CEO asked to walk through it building-by-building, the records didn't align cleanly enough for her to defend a single building's number in under 60 seconds.
>
> The CEO didn't accuse her of anything. He just said: *"Let me know when you can show me the receipts."*
>
> He ended the QBR at minute 12. Sara had just made a withdrawal from the credibility account she couldn't deposit back into for two more quarters. The renewal conversation 60 days later was uphill.
>
> **What went wrong:** Sara made a claim her records could not defend in real time. The 12% was true in spirit but unprovable on demand — and a QBR demand IS real time.
>
> **What Pillar 8 says:** *Unrecorded work is unprovable work.* The corollary: *Imprecisely recorded work is imprecisely provable work.* If you cannot trace a claim back to a defensible record in under 60 seconds while the CEO watches, the claim is not ready to make.
>
> **The three Pillar 8 questions** every Operator must answer BEFORE the QBR:
>
> 1. Can I support every claim with a specific record the customer can audit?
> 2. Was the record created BY the work, or reconstructed AFTER it?
> 3. If the customer's lawyer or auditor asked for proof of any claim, could I produce it inside 60 seconds?
>
> If any answer is no, the claim doesn't make the QBR.
>
> **The recovery move:** if you're already mid-QBR and the CEO challenges a claim you can't defend, the correct response is the hardest sentence in the operator's toolkit:
>
> > *"You're right to push. Let me come back with the building-level records. Can I do it by Friday?"*
>
> Then you do — and you take the credibility deposit for the disclosure even though you took the withdrawal for the miss.

The Pillar 8 mistake is the only Common Mistake in this lesson with no "sounds like / upgrade" pair. That's intentional. The mistake is not a sentence — it is a missing artifact. The fix lives in the prep, not the delivery.

## ELEMENT 6 — Vocabulary

### 90-Second Rule

- **Definition.** Any finding, however complex, must have a faithful 90-second version a CEO can absorb between meetings. If the Operator cannot produce it, the analysis is unfinished.
- **Why it matters.** Compression is comprehension's proof. Inability to compress is inability to think clearly.
- **Example.** Jay's 90-second version of Northwest's renewal case: "You pay $14,500. We've delivered $88,000 in verified savings this year. Competitor is $11,300 but can't show you compliance records. You'd save $38,400/year and lose audit defense worth more. Stay."
- **Common mistake.** Mistaking the 90-second version for the only version. The long version exists too — it is the appendix, not the opener.

### Credibility Account

- **Definition.** The running balance of an Operator's trust with a CEO, deposited and withdrawn through every QBR and major recommendation.
- **Why it matters.** A high credibility account makes future recommendations approved in the hallway. A low one means every recommendation gets re-litigated.
- **Example.** Three years into the relationship, April's CEO had stopped questioning her recommendations. He referenced her by name when other vendors challenged her advice. The account had compounded.
- **Common mistake.** Spending the account on small wins. Don't deposit toward a confrontation you haven't had yet. Save the credibility for the big call.

### Decision-Forcing Function

- **Definition.** A conversation whose explicit purpose is to produce decisions, not transmit information. The QBR is the canonical example.
- **Why it matters.** Most CSM meetings are information-forcing. They produce slides, not decisions. Operators force decisions.
- **Example.** A QBR that opens "three decisions by the end of this conversation" has named itself a decision-forcing function and bound itself to produce them.
- **Common mistake.** Calling a meeting a QBR when no decision is required. That makes it a status meeting wearing a QBR badge.

### Inaction Cost (Cost of Inaction)

- **Definition.** The dollar value of what continues to be lost each month while a recommendation is not approved or executed.
- **Why it matters.** "We should fix this" loses to the status quo. "This is costing you $4,100 a month while we discuss it" wins. The inaction cost is the only argument the status quo reliably loses to.
- **Example.** Pioneer recommends a closeout workflow change. Inaction cost: "$5,300/month in stretched AR while we wait." That makes Friday's decision feel urgent.
- **Common mistake.** Skipping the inaction cost because it feels confrontational. Without it, the recommendation is a suggestion.

### Loop Closure

- **Definition.** The act of reporting back on every prediction made in the prior QBR — what was predicted, what verified, and the credibility implications of each.
- **Why it matters.** Loop closure is the credibility deposit. Operators who close loops compound trust. Operators who don't reset their credibility every QBR to zero.
- **Example.** "Three predictions last quarter. One landed exactly. One overshot. One undershot. Net $88,000 in verified savings. Here is the record."
- **Common mistake.** Closing only the wins. Honesty about failures is what makes the wins credible.

### Verification Date

- **Definition.** A specific calendar date at which a metric will be re-measured against the pre-declared expectation of an Operator's recommendation.
- **Why it matters.** Per Thesis P9: an intervention without a verification date is a hypothesis that was never tested. The date is the closure mechanism.
- **Example.** "Change supply ordering. Predicted result: $4,300/month savings. Verification: March 15. If we hit, change stays. If not, reverse."
- **Common mistake.** Setting verification dates and not honoring them. Verification only works when it actually happens.

```
   Loop closed at next QBR ───► back to top
```

**What this teaches:** the QBR is one stage of a longer loop. The 30-minute conversation is the visible part. Most of the work is upstream (capture, AI-augmented analysis, pricing) and downstream (verification, loop closure).

## What moves a QBR from "delivered" to "transformative"

Three properties separate a 7/10 QBR from a 9/10 QBR. They are practice-able.

### 1. The 8-minute prep test

A great QBR feels effortless because the prep was ruthless. The test:

> Can the Operator name, from memory, before walking in:
> - The three vitals trending the wrong way?
> - The single biggest dollar gap in the customer's portfolio?
> - The state of last QBR's three predictions?
> - The three recommendations they'll make today, including the inaction cost of each?

If the answer is yes to all four, the Operator is ready. If the answer is no on any, they haven't compressed the analysis enough yet.

### 2. The named hostile scenario

Before delivery, the Operator names the most hostile reaction the CEO could plausibly have, and rehearses the response.

> "If the CEO says 'these numbers don't look right,' I will respond by inviting verification directly: 'Let's pull QuickBooks and PioneerOps side by side and trace one customer to the line item.'"

The named scenario is rarely the actual scenario. But rehearsing for the worst case produces calm in the actual case (Mindset 3 — calm inside ambiguity).

### 3. The 24-hour follow-up doc

The QBR is not finished at minute 30. Within 24 hours, the Operator sends:

- A one-page summary of the three recommendations
- The three inaction costs and effort estimates
- The three verification dates
- One line of "next QBR is scheduled for [date]"

This is the artifact the CEO references when their bookkeeper or their second-in-command questions one of the decisions. Without it, the decision dies between the QBR and execution.

## How AI is used in QBR prep (Pillar 5 — division of labor)

The QBR is a high-leverage place to use AI well. The division of labor:

| Stage | AI's job | Operator's job |
|---|---|---|
| Data assembly | Pull 90 days of records across PioneerOps + QuickBooks; assemble trends; flag anomalies | Decide which trends matter; reject the ones that are noise |
| Pattern identification | Cluster events, surface correlations across vitals | Validate the cluster against field reality; throw out the false patterns |
| Draft synthesis | Write a 3-page first draft of the QBR narrative | Rewrite into Operator voice; verify every number; cut 60% of the text |
| Recommendation generation | Propose 5–8 candidate recommendations with rough cost estimates | Pick the three. Tighten the math. Own the recommendation. |
| 90-second compression | Draft a 90-second version | Rewrite until it actually sounds like the Operator on a hallway |
| Verification follow-up | Track verification dates, send reminders, draft check-in notes | Make the verification call. Read the result. Decide what to do. |

**What AI does NOT do:**

- Make recommendations on its own
- Sign the work
- Speak to the customer
- Decide what matters
- Carry the responsibility

The Operator's signature on the QBR is the entire credibility account. AI accelerates the prep. AI does not get to the meeting.

> Operators who use AI to produce more QBR slides have misunderstood the doctrine. The constraint was never slide volume.

## How a QBR connects to Pillar 8 (Work Becomes the Record)

A QBR's credibility comes entirely from the records beneath it.

> "Your inspector coverage was 78%" is a claim.
>
> "Your inspector coverage was 78% — here are the 248 records we have, and here are the 12 buildings missing inspections in the period" is a defensible position.

The work must have become the record before the QBR can land. The QBR is the closing argument of a 90-day evidence-gathering period that started the day after the last QBR.

**Three Pillar 8 questions to answer before any QBR:**

1. Can I support every claim with a specific record the customer can audit?
2. Was the record created BY the work (Pillar 8) or reconstructed AFTER it?
3. If the customer's lawyer or auditor asked for proof of any claim, could I produce it inside 60 seconds?

If any answer is no, the QBR is not ready. Tighten the record before the conversation.

## ELEMENT 8 — Exercise

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Task** | Build and deliver a complete QBR for one of your owned accounts, using the 30-minute spine. Record the delivery. |

| **Success looks like** | Trainer watches the recording and says: "I would let you deliver this to a real customer CEO tomorrow." |

### Time breakdown (honest)

The exercise was previously scoped at 135 minutes. It does not actually fit in 135 minutes for any operator delivering it for the first time. Honest timing:

| Stage | Time |
|---|---|
| **Part 1 — Build the artifact (2 hours)** | |
| Data assembly (PioneerOps + QuickBooks 90-day pull) | 30 min |
| Pattern identification + Pillar 7 pricing + draft synthesis | 45 min |
| Compression to 6-slide / 2-page artifact + verify every number | 30 min |
| Rehearse 90-second compression version | 15 min |
| **Part 2 — Deliver + close the loop (~65 min)** | |
| Delivery (recorded, 30 minutes hard cap) | 30 min |
| 24-hour follow-up doc (drafted same day) | 20 min |
| Self-review against the rubric | 15 min |

Operators who try to compress Part 1 below 2 hours will produce shaky QBRs. Operators who try to extend Part 2 beyond 65 minutes are over-thinking the delivery — the prep was supposed to do that work.

For the trainer: schedule Part 1 and Part 2 on separate days. Same-day delivery on top of same-day prep produces worse outcomes than rest.

### Required structure for the QBR artifact

```
1. State of the business
   - Name 3 vitals (out of the 6)
   - For each: trend (up/down/flat), one sentence, one number
   - Cite the underlying record

2. Loop closure
   - Last QBR's predictions (full list, including failures)
   - Verified results (full list)
   - Net financial impact ($ this quarter, $ annualized)

3. Three recommendations (one each, in this format)
   - What we recommend (one sentence — specific action)
   - Why (cause + the metric supporting it)
   - Cost of inaction ($/month, arithmetic visible)
   - Effort to act (hours, dollars, or both)
   - Expected result (named metric, named delta)
   - Verification date (calendar date)

4. Close
   - Recap three asks in one sentence
   - Named decision date (when the CEO commits)
```

### Required 24-hour follow-up doc

A single page sent within 24 hours of the QBR. Contains:

- The three recommendations restated
- The three inaction costs
- The three verification dates
- The next QBR date
- One line: "Reach out if anything in the above doesn't match what you remember from our conversation."

That last line is a Pillar 8 record — it makes the CEO's response (or non-response) part of the record. Silence becomes consent. Disagreement becomes documented disagreement.

### What the 24-hour follow-up doc must NOT contain

The follow-up doc is a Pillar 8 record. Soft language corrupts the record. The discipline:

| Don't write | Why it fails | Write instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Sorry for taking your time today." | Apology language anchors the conversation as imposition, not value. | Open directly with the three recommendations. |
| "Aiming to have results by May 15." | "Aiming" hedges the verification date. Hedged dates evaporate. | "Verification: May 15." Period. |
| "Let me know if you have any questions!" | Invites questions instead of producing decisions. | "Reach out if anything above doesn't match what you remember." |
| "Looking forward to our next conversation!" | Closing pleasantry adds no information; CEO scans past it. | End on the next QBR date, full stop. |
| A 4-paragraph preamble before the recommendations | Buries the lede. CEO scans for the asks; preamble delays them. | Lead with the three recommendations. Preamble is optional appendix. |
| Multiple pages of supporting math | The doc is for the CEO, not the bookkeeper. Math belongs in the deck. | Keep to one page. Numbers as bullets, not paragraphs. |
| Smiley faces, exclamation points (more than 1), informal slang | Erodes the doc's status as a Pillar 8 record. | Professional tone. A QBR doc gets read three times in the next 18 months. |

**The 30-second scan test:** the CEO should be able to scan the doc in 30 seconds and walk away knowing: the three recommendations, the three inaction costs, the three verification dates, and the next QBR date. If they can't, the doc has too much chrome.

### Submission

Send the artifact + the recording + the self-review to your trainer. Be ready to defend every number on every slide.

## ELEMENT 9 — Role-play

### Scenario A — "The CEO who came to leave"

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **Scenario name** | Renewal at risk — competitor undercut |
| **Pillar / Competency** | Pillar 05 · 05.3 + Pillar 07 · 07.1 (CEO conversation) + 07.4 (Pushback) |
| **Format** | Live role-play, recorded |
| **Time** | 45 min (25 min run + 20 min debrief) |

### Setup — the customer

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Company | (Fictional) Sound View Janitorial |
| Size | 26 employees, ~$2.6M revenue |
| Region | Tacoma–Olympia corridor |
| Service mix | 75% recurring nightly, 15% specialty, 10% day porter |
| Owner style | Pragmatic, direct, owner-operator, second-generation |
| Pioneer tenure | 16 months — at Adoption stage |
| Setup today | 90 days from renewal · received a competitor proposal at 19% less per month |
| Bug in the trainer's prep | The competitor's product cannot do scored compliance inspections — but the trainer doesn't tell the operator that. The Operator must do the homework. |

### The PioneerOps data the operator has

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Owned account contract value | $13,800/month |
| Competitor proposal | $11,200/month |
| Verified savings (last 12 months) | $96,000 portfolio |
| Inspection records (last 12 months) | 264 |
| Buildings under inspection | 100% (zero exposure) |
| Recent failed prediction (intentional gotcha) | Predicted Q2 rate increase at 8%, landed 5% |
| Last QBR's three recs | All approved, two verified positive, one undershot |

### The operator's task

25 minutes. The CEO is going to bring up the competitor proposal in the first three minutes. The Operator must:

1. NOT defend the price
2. Use loop closure to deposit credibility
3. Surface the Operational Exposure vital (the competitor cannot defend compliance)
4. Make three recommendations regardless of the renewal threat
5. Leave the conversation with a renewal commitment OR a clear next step toward one

### The trainer's role

Play the CEO. Style:

- Pragmatic, money-focused
- Has a relationship with the Operator but treats this conversation transactionally
- Default move when challenged: "What does that mean for me?"
- Will bring up the competitor in the first three minutes
- Will challenge any unsupported number

### Hostile-but-fair questions

Pick 2–3 to land:

1. "What does Pioneer cost me total this year? Don't round."
2. "I have a proposal in hand for $2,600 less a month. Talk me out of it."
3. "You missed your Q2 rate-increase prediction by 3 points. Why should I trust this next prediction?"
4. "What can the competitor NOT do that you can?"
5. "If I told you I'm staying, what would your next recommendation be?"

### The trap

The trap is to defend Pioneer's price. The Operator who says "but you get X, Y, Z features for that price" has lost.

The right move is to make the renewal a business decision, not a price negotiation. The price gap is real. The value gap is bigger. Show the math.

### Success criteria

- [ ] Operator did NOT lead with thanks for the partnership
- [ ] Operator did NOT defend Pioneer's price
- [ ] Operator DID open with three decisions to make
- [ ] Operator named the failed prediction before being asked
- [ ] Operator named at least one specific record (number of inspections, specific buildings, etc.)
- [ ] Operator handled the competitor question by reframing to value, not price
- [ ] Operator surfaced the Operational Exposure vital
- [ ] Operator made three recommendations regardless of renewal threat
- [ ] Operator left with a concrete next step (commitment, signed renewal, or scheduled follow-up by named date)
- [ ] Operator did NOT pitch any new module or upsell

### Debrief structure (20 min)

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:04 | Operator self-rates against the criteria above |
| 0:04–0:10 | Trainer feedback: one keep + one change + one moment that surprised them |
| 0:10–0:16 | Replay the moment the CEO said "I have a proposal in hand for less" — how could the operator have responded differently? Re-run that 60 seconds. |
| 0:16–0:20 | Operator commits to one specific change for the next QBR they deliver |

### Scenario B — "The CEO who is doing well"

Same customer, same data — but assume the CEO is enthusiastic, not skeptical. The renewal is not threatened. The operator must now do a HARDER thing: identify the next expansion, propose it in the QBR without it feeling like a pitch, and convert engagement into commitment.

### Scenario C — "The CEO with the CFO"

Same customer, but the CEO brings their bookkeeper to challenge methodology. The Operator must hold the line on methodology without losing the CEO's attention.

### Scenario D — "The 5-minute version"

The same QBR, but the CEO can only give 5 minutes. The Operator must compress the entire 30-minute spine. This is the 90-second rule applied at extreme.

### A note on the role-play hierarchy

Scenario A is the certification-grade scenario. Scenarios B–D are stretches for advanced Operators. An Operator who can pass A is Operator II-ready on this competency. An Operator who can pass all four is approaching Senior tier.

## ELEMENT 10 — Quiz

10 questions. Score 7+ to pass.

### Q1 — Definition (short answer)

In one sentence, what is a QBR?

**Sample strong answer:** A 30-minute conversation with a customer CEO where the Operator turns a quarter of operational truth into three priced executive decisions.

**What we look for:** time discipline (30 min), executive audience (CEO), decision-forcing (not status), three (not seven), priced (Pillar 7).

---

### Q2 — Structure (multiple choice)

Which of the following is NOT part of the QBR spine?

- a) State of the Business
- b) Loop Closure (predictions vs verified)
- c) Three Recommendations
- d) Demo of the new product features
- e) Close with decision date

**Answer:** d

**Why it matters:** Feature talk corrupts the QBR. It belongs in a separate pitch conversation, never inside the QBR.

---

### Q3 — Credibility (short answer — 2 sentences)

A failed prediction should be addressed in a QBR in what way?

**Sample strong answer:** The Operator names the failed prediction first, before any wins. Owning the miss is what makes the wins credible — the credibility deposit is bigger from honesty about failure than from celebration of success.

**What we look for:** Operator names the failure proactively, before the customer asks. This is a doctrine-level habit.

---

### Q4 — Math (calculation)

Customer's inspection coverage dropped from 96% to 73%. They have 60 buildings. Each uncovered building represents roughly $400/month in compliance exposure (estimated by the Operator). What is the inaction cost?

**Answer:**

- Uncovered buildings: 60 × (96% − 73%) = 60 × 23% ≈ 14 buildings
- Inaction cost: 14 × $400 = **$5,600/month** (or ~$67,200/year)

**What we look for:** the Operator does this math during the QBR, not in advance. The CEO sees the arithmetic.

---

### Q5 — Recommendation structure (short answer — list)

What are the six components every QBR recommendation must contain?

**Answer:** (1) What — specific action, (2) Why — cause + supporting metric, (3) Cost of inaction in dollars, (4) Effort to act, (5) Expected result (named metric, named delta), (6) Verification date.

**What we look for:** the operator can recite this from memory. It is the recommendation skeleton.

---

### Q6 — Common mistake (multiple choice)

A "naked problem" presented in a QBR is dangerous because:

- a) It distracts from the wins
- b) It delegates the diagnosis upward to the CEO, who shouldn't be doing the Operator's job
- c) It might offend the CEO
- d) It's not technically a problem

**Answer:** b

**Why it matters:** the Operator's job is to bring the problem + the price + the recommendation. A naked problem is unfinished work.

---

### Q7 — Scenario (scenario response — 3 sentences)

The CEO opens the QBR by saying: "Look, I'm seriously considering switching to a competitor that costs $2,800 less a month. Let's get into it."

What is your opening response?

**Sample strong answer:**

"Then today's three decisions are different from what I planned. Decision one: stay or go — I'll show you the math by the end. Decision two: what to do about the $42,000 in verified savings I'll walk you through next. Decision three: whether to lock in next quarter's recommendations regardless. Let's start with the savings, because the rest builds on that."

**What we look for:**

- Operator does NOT defend the price
- Operator reframes the conversation to decisions, not negotiation
- Operator commits to addressing the renewal explicitly (not avoiding it)
- Operator's energy is calm, not panicked

---

### Q8 — Verification (short answer)

What is a pre-declared verification date, and what does it prevent?

**Sample strong answer:** A specific calendar date, written down BEFORE the action is taken, on which the metric will be re-measured against the expected result. It prevents retroactive claims that "the action worked" — without a pre-declared expectation, every outcome can be wrapped into the original intent.

**What we look for:** understanding that the prediction must be written down BEFORE, not after. This is doctrine (Thesis P9).

---

### Q9 — AI division of labor (multiple choice)

Which of the following is the OPERATOR's job, not AI's, in QBR prep?

- a) Pulling 90 days of records from PioneerOps + QuickBooks
- b) Clustering events to surface correlations
- c) Drafting a 3-page first synthesis
- d) Deciding which three recommendations to bring to the CEO
- e) Tracking verification dates and sending reminders

**Answer:** d

**Why it matters:** Recommendations carry the Operator's signature. AI proposes; the Operator decides.

### Q10 — Compression (judgment)

You have 90 seconds with the CEO in a hallway. You need to convey the entire content of a 30-minute QBR you've prepared. Demonstrate the compression.

**Sample strong answer (in 90 seconds, written or spoken):**

> "Three things. Your portfolio's gross margin is up 4 points this quarter — $42K we hadn't seen before — because of the supply ordering change and Mike's reassignment. We undershot the rate increase prediction at Acme by 2 points; my call, I should have predicted the pushback. Three asks: re-price six customers (inaction cost $14K/month), tighten closeout (inaction cost $5K/month in stretched AR), cover the audit-exposure gap on healthcare accounts (indefensible position worth a contract). I need your decision on these three by Friday. Long version is in the doc — should I send it?"

**What we look for:**

- Lands in 90 seconds (read aloud)
- Hits the structure: loop closure, three asks, decision date
- Names the inaction costs
- Names the failure before the wins (credibility deposit)
- Ends with a specific ask of the CEO

This question is the lesson's hardest. If the Operator can deliver this compression cleanly, they are at level 3.

#### Scoring rubric for Q10 (mandatory — all five required for pass)

The 90-second compression is the lesson's diagnostic centerpiece. Two trainers must score this question identically. Use this rubric exactly.

| Criterion | Pass bar | How to verify |
|---|---|---|
| **Time** | 80–95 seconds (timed strictly) | Stopwatch. Under 80 = rushed and lacking substance. Over 95 = compression failed. |
| **Opens with three asks** | First sentence names exactly three decisions to be made | Listen for a number ("three") in the first 8 seconds. |
| **Names a failure before any win** | At least one failed prediction is named before any verified win | Catch the sequencing. Wins first = fail this criterion. |
| **Carries at least one inaction cost in dollars** | At least one specific dollar number tied to inaction | Listen for "$X/month" or "$Y per year" with a named cause. |
| **Ends with specific CEO ask + date** | Closes with a specific decision request and a calendar date | "I need your decision on these three by Friday" or equivalent. |

**Scoring rule:** all five must hit. Four out of five = re-attempt next week. Three or fewer = lesson needs re-delivery before re-test.

**Calibration note for trainers:** before grading any operator on this question, the trainer should deliver their OWN 90-second version to a peer and have it scored against this rubric. A trainer who cannot pass the rubric should not be grading it.
