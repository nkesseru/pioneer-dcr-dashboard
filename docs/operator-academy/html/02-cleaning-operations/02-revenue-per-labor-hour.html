ELEMENT 1 — Plain-English Explanation

What RPLH is

For every hour someone cleans, how much does that hour earn?

Take one customer. In one month:

  • They pay the cleaning company $4,200 (the invoice)
  • Cleaners spend 100 hours at their building that month

$4,200 ÷ 100 hours = $42 per labor hour.

That is Revenue Per Labor Hour. RPLH.

The formula

                  Customer revenue (last 30 days)
   RPLH   =   ─────────────────────────────────────
                  Customer labor hours (last 30 days)

Simple division. The hard part is not the math. The hard part is what to do once you see the number.

How it scales to the whole company

For the company average, add up every customer's revenue and every customer's labor hours, then divide:

                  Total revenue across all customers
   Company    =   ──────────────────────────────────
   Avg RPLH       Total labor hours across all customers

This is a weighted average. Big customers count more than small ones. A small customer with crazy RPLH does not swing the company number much.

A 30-second example

Customer Monthly Revenue Monthly Labor Hours RPLH
Riverside Office $5,040 60 $84
Dental Plaza SE $4,400 100 $44
Both combined $9,440 160 $59

The company average ($59) hides the truth. Riverside is great. Dental Plaza is not. The operator who reports only the average is hiding the action.

ELEMENT 2 — Why It Matters

A cleaning company makes money one hour at a time

Every labor hour costs real dollars: wages, payroll tax, supplies, and (eventually) overhead. Every labor hour also earns specific revenue from a specific customer.

If RPLH on a customer is below the cost-to-clean break-even, the company is paying to clean that customer. The relationship looks healthy on paper. The customer is friendly. The contract has been in place for years.

But every hour spent there is a hole in the bank.

The four zones every customer falls into

   $$$    EXCELLENT   ── well above target ──   subsidizes the rest
                                                of the company

   $$     ON TARGET   ── healthy margin   ──   the goal for every
                                                customer

   $      BELOW TARGET ── thin margin    ──   one callout away from
                                                losing money

   $      BLEEDING    ── below break-even ──  paying to clean

Every cleaning customer sits in one of those four zones. The operator's job is to know which zone every customer is in.

Why CEOs love this number

CEOs translate everything into time or money in their heads. RPLH does both for them automatically.

  • "Your RPLH is $48 against a $62 target."
  • "On your current labor volume, that gap equals $14,000/month in left-on-the-table revenue."
  • "Over 12 months that is $168,000 — about what a route manager costs."

That second and third sentence is what the operator brings. The CEO did the rest.

What happens when CEOs cannot see it

They guess. They quote a margin number ("we make 8%") that they computed two years ago and assume still holds. They renew bad contracts because the customer has been with them forever. They cut wages instead of cutting bad customers — and lose their best techs.

The cost of not seeing RPLH is not just lost margin. It is lost techs, lost customers, and a CEO who runs the company by feel.

ELEMENT 4 — Customer Example

How Pioneer customer ACME Cleaning used RPLH to double their margin

The setup. ACME Cleaning is a Pioneer customer running 28 active accounts in the Spokane area. Owner had been quoting "we make 8% margin" for two years. CFO agreed with him. They had not actually computed it from the ground up since 2024.

The first look. Pioneer's operator pulled the Customer Economics card with the ACME owner on a Tuesday. Numbers:

RPLH
Company average $51
Target (configured by ACME) $58
Gap -$7

Then the breakdown:

Customer RPLH
Top 1 $79
Top 2 $74
Top 3 $71
... ...
Bottom 3 $42
Bottom 2 $39
Bottom 1 $34

The owner went pale. The bottom three were three of his oldest accounts. He had never raised rates on them. The math said he was paying to clean those buildings.

What ACME did next.

Week Action
Week 1 Listed the bottom 6 customers; built a per-customer case for each
Week 2–4 Operator helped draft the conversation for each one
Week 5–10 ACME owner ran six rate-increase conversations
Week 11 3 customers accepted the full 11% rate increase
Week 12 2 customers negotiated to 7% increases
Week 14 1 customer churned — owner did the math, decided he was better off without them

The result, 6 months later.

Metric Before After
Company average RPLH $51 $56
Actual margin (not the quoted 8%) ~8% ~18%
Lost revenue from the one churn -$3,400/month offset 4× by gains
Annual margin gain — About $190,000

The owner's quote, when April followed up at the next QBR:

"I had been guessing for two years. I didn't know I was guessing until you showed me this."

That is the moment a Pioneer customer becomes a Pioneer advocate.

ELEMENT 5 — Common Mistake

Operators talk about the company average

The biggest, most natural mistake. Easy to make. Costly in the conversation.

Mistake: the operator leads with the company average RPLH and the target gap.

Why operators fall into it: the average is the most prominent number on the Customer Economics card. It is right at the top. It looks important.

Cost: the company average is the least actionable number on the card. Owners cannot do anything about an "average." They can only do things about specific customers.

The better move

Always lead with the breakdown.

Bad opening Good opening
"Your company average RPLH is $51 against a $58 target." "Three of your customers are at $42, $39, and $34. Your target is $58. Let me show you what that means."

The good opening puts a name and a number on the action. The bad opening is a number an owner cannot do anything with on a Tuesday morning.

Other common mistakes

Mistake Why it's wrong Better move
Comparing one customer's RPLH to another customer's RPLH directly Different service types have different cost structures Compare each to the company target, not to each other
Using "profitability" interchangeably with RPLH RPLH does not include overhead Call it Customer Economics or RPLH. Never call it profit.
Recommending a rate increase before checking why the customer is below target Sometimes the cause is scope drift the customer never asked for Diagnose first. Scope-fix is often cheaper than rate-fix.
Showing the bottom 3 list without context Owners get defensive about long-time customers Pair the bottom 3 with the math: "this one specifically is costing you $X/month"

ELEMENT 6 — Vocabulary

Revenue Per Labor Hour (RPLH)

  • Definition. Revenue from a customer divided by hours of cleaning labor spent on that customer in the same period.
  • Why it matters. The single most important number in commercial cleaning. Below target = losing money on that customer.
  • Real example. Customer pays $4,200 in a month. Cleaners spent 100 hours there. RPLH = $42.
  • Common misunderstanding. Confusing it with profitability. RPLH ignores overhead. We call it Customer Economics, not profit.

Customer Economics

  • Definition. A complete view of which customers are earning money for the cleaning company and which are losing money — measured by RPLH against a target.
  • Why it matters. The owner's truth machine. Without it, owners run on stories. With it, they run on numbers.
  • Real example. ACME Cleaning's Customer Economics card showed 6 of 28 customers below target. The owner addressed all 6 inside 90 days.
  • Common misunderstanding. Treating it as a finance dashboard. It is an operations dashboard. The decisions it drives are operational (pricing, scope, customer mix).

Target RPLH

  • Definition. The RPLH number the cleaning company decides is the minimum for a healthy customer. Set by the owner. Adjustable.
  • Why it matters. Without a target, the company has no way to say "this customer is below the bar." The target turns RPLH into a decision.
  • Real example. Pioneer's default target is $62. ACME set theirs at $58 because their wage band is lower. Both are valid.
  • Common misunderstanding. Thinking the target is universal. It is per-company. It changes as wages, supplies, and contracts evolve.

All three terms are also in the Operator Glossary. Add new terms there as you teach them.

What moves RPLH — drivers

The operator must know what makes RPLH go up and what makes it go down. Without this, recommendations are guesses.

What makes RPLH RISE

Driver What happens Example
Rate increase Customer pays more for the same scope Raise monthly fee 8%; labor stays the same; RPLH up 8%
Scope reduction Customer pays the same; cleaners do less Drop biweekly window cleaning; -4 hrs/month at no revenue loss; RPLH rises
Productivity gain Same scope, less labor Tech learns a 20-minute time-saver; same revenue, fewer hours, RPLH rises
Add-on service Customer pays more for additional work; labor rises in proportion or less Add quarterly deep clean; revenue up more than labor; weighted RPLH rises
Route consolidation Travel and setup time drops when customer is grouped with nearby accounts Same revenue, fewer effective labor hours, RPLH rises
Tech assignment Faster tech replaces slower tech on the route Same revenue, fewer hours, RPLH rises

What makes RPLH FALL

Driver What happens Example
Scope creep Customer asks for "just one more thing"; cleaners do it; no extra revenue "Can you also wipe down the conference room?" — 15 min/night for free, RPLH falls
Re-cleans Tech does the work twice (quality issue, inspector failed it) Double the labor; same revenue; RPLH halves on that shift
Callouts and reschedules A tech calls out; a manager covers; overtime kicks in Same revenue; higher cost per hour; effective RPLH drops
Wage increase without rate increase Tech wages go up; customer contract does not Same revenue; higher labor cost; effective RPLH drops
Supply expansion More expensive consumables used; revenue stays put Same revenue; higher cost per hour; effective RPLH drops
Time-card padding Tech logs more time than was worked Same revenue; reported labor up; RPLH drops on paper even if reality is fine

What an operator does with this

When a customer's RPLH is below target, the operator's first question is why. Not "which lever should we pull." First diagnosis, then prescription.

The wrong move: jump straight to a rate increase.

The right move:

  1. Look at scope vs contract — has scope crept?
  2. Look at labor hours trend — are hours rising while revenue is flat?
  3. Look at re-cleans — has quality slipped?
  4. Look at tech assignment — has a slower tech been on the route lately?
  5. THEN consider rate increase if none of the above explains the gap.

A rate increase is the last lever, not the first.

How PioneerOps changes the math

The reason the operator can have this conversation at all is that PioneerOps produces the numbers. Without PioneerOps, even sophisticated cleaning companies do not have customer-level RPLH visibility.

Where each number comes from

Number Source in PioneerOps
Customer revenue (last 30 days) Pulled from QuickBooks Online invoices, joined by customer name
Customer labor hours (last 30 days) Computed from pioneer_service_sessions — cleaning labor only (no inspection, no supply-station, no overhead)
RPLH Daily sync (07:00 PT) writes customer_economics/current
Target Editable in pioneer_config/customer_economics.target_rplh (no redeploy)
Top 3 / Bottom 3 / Opportunities Computed server-side; sorted by absolute gap

Why the math is honest

Three reasons most cleaning-company-internal RPLH spreadsheets are wrong:

  1. Labor hours mix overhead with production. Many spreadsheets include hiring time, training time, management time, supply runs. We exclude all of that. Only cleaning labor counts.
  2. Customer mapping is broken. Names in QuickBooks don't match names in the time-tracking system. We use customer_aliases to join them cleanly.
  3. Min-hours threshold is missing. Customers with under 10 labor hours produce noisy RPLH (a one-hour shift on a $300 invoice = $300/hour, which is meaningless). We exclude them. Threshold is configurable.

What this means in the customer conversation

The operator can say:

"These numbers are computed from your QuickBooks invoices and your team's actual clock-in time. Cleaning labor only. We exclude inspection and overhead so the comparison across customers is fair. Anyone below 10 hours is excluded so the math isn't noisy."

That paragraph wins the credibility battle in 30 seconds. Cleaning company owners have heard sloppy math before. Honest math is rare and memorable.

What PioneerOps does NOT do (be honest about this)

  • Does not include overhead allocation. We call this Customer Economics, not profit.
  • Does not account for differences in service type (recurring vs specialty). Some customers will appear "low" because their service mix is naturally labor-heavy.
  • Does not catch time-card padding. If a tech logs more time than worked, RPLH looks artificially low.
  • Does not include AR risk. A high-RPLH customer who never pays is not actually high-RPLH.

A good operator names these limits before the customer notices them. It builds trust.

The operator's task

15-minute conversation with the owner. Walk through Customer Economics. Get the owner to commit to looking harder at the bottom 3 within the next two weeks.

The trainer's role

Play the owner. Style:

  • Direct, no patience for vendor talk
  • Believes he knows his business better than any software
  • Has a personal relationship with each of the bottom 3 customers (they've been with him for years)
  • Default move when challenged: "your numbers must be wrong"

Hostile-but-fair questions the trainer asks

Pick 2–3 to land during the conversation:

  1. "How do I know your math is right? I've been doing this 20 years and these numbers don't match what I'd expect."
  2. "You're telling me to raise rates on three customers who have been with me since my dad ran the company. That's not how this works."
  3. "If RPLH was this important, why hasn't every cleaning company done this already?"
  4. "What if I just cut wages instead? Same result, fewer relationships to ruin."

The trap

The trap is to defend the math instead of inviting the owner to verify it himself. The operator who says "trust the numbers" loses. The operator who says "open QuickBooks with me right now and we'll trace it line by line" wins.

Success criteria

  • Operator did NOT lead with the company average
  • Operator named at least one specific bottom customer by name
  • Operator translated the gap into dollars or time, not just a ratio
  • Operator handled the pushback without folding OR getting defensive
  • Operator left the conversation with a concrete next step (15-min follow-up next week, owner commits to looking)
  • Operator named at least one limit of the Customer Economics math before the owner pointed it out

Debrief structure (15 min)

Time Activity
0:00–0:03 Operator self-rates: what worked, what didn't
0:03–0:08 Trainer feedback (one keep, one change)
0:08–0:13 Replay the inflection point — the moment the owner pushed back
0:13–0:15 Operator commits to one specific change next week

Variations

After the operator passes the base scenario, run these:

  • Friendly owner. Same data, owner is curious and engaged. Operator must move past "education" into "action" — what does the owner DO this week?
  • Hostile owner. Same data, owner has already decided RPLH is "an HR move to cut wages." Reset the conversation.
  • Confused CFO. Same data, owner has brought their CFO who wants to debate methodology. Operator handles both without losing the owner.

ELEMENT 10 — Quiz

10 questions. Score 7+ to pass. The trainer reviews missed questions during coaching.

Q1 — Definition (multiple choice)

Revenue Per Labor Hour is calculated by dividing:

  • a) Customer revenue by total company labor hours
  • b) Customer revenue by that customer's cleaning labor hours
  • c) Profit by labor hours
  • d) Customer revenue by all hours including overhead

Answer: b

Why it matters: The denominator is customer-specific cleaning labor — not company labor, not total payroll, not "all hours."

Q2 — Calculation (math)

A customer pays $5,400 monthly. Your cleaners log 120 cleaning hours at their site that month. The company target is $60. What is their RPLH and gap to target?

Answer: RPLH = $45. Gap = -$15.

What we look for: Right number AND right sign on the gap (negative = below). Quick math, not a spreadsheet.

Q3 — Driver identification (multiple choice)

A customer's RPLH dropped from $58 to $46 over three months. Their contract rate has not changed. What is most likely the cause?

  • a) Wage increase across the cleaning company
  • b) Scope creep — they've been asking for additional services without paying
  • c) AR aging — they're paying slower
  • d) Inflation in supply costs

Answer: b

Why it matters: The metric is REVENUE per labor hour. AR aging and supply costs don't affect the ratio. Wage increases don't appear in this metric directly. Scope creep adds labor without adding revenue.

Q4 — Diagnosis (short answer)

A customer's RPLH is below target. List the four things you'd check, in order, before recommending a rate increase.

Sample strong answer:

  1. Scope vs contract — has scope crept?
  2. Labor hours trend — are hours rising while revenue is flat?
  3. Re-cleans — has quality slipped?
  4. Tech assignment — has a slower tech been on the route?

What we look for: Diagnostic mindset. Operators who jump to rate increase as a first move fail this question.

Q5 — Customer conversation (short answer — 2 sentences)

How would you open a Customer Economics conversation with an owner who is seeing the data for the first time?

Sample strong answer:

"Three of your customers are at $34, $39, and $42. Your target is $58. Let me show you what that means in dollars per month, and which one is most fixable this quarter."

What we look for:

  • Specific customer numbers, NOT the company average
  • Translation into business language (dollars per month)
  • A forward path (which one to fix)

Common weak answer: "Your company average RPLH is $51 against a $58 target." (Leads with the least actionable number.)

Q6 — Scenario (3 sentences)

You're meeting with the ACME Cleaning owner. Bottom customer is at $34 RPLH, $3,800/month revenue, 112 labor hours. Target is $58. Owner wants to know what to do.

What's your recommended action AND what's the required monthly revenue increase to hit target?

Answer:

  • Required revenue at target: $58 × 112 hrs = $6,496
  • Required increase: $6,496 − $3,800 = $2,696 / month (about 71% increase)
  • Recommended action: this gap is so large that a single rate increase is unlikely to work. The right move is a scope-and-pricing conversation: reduce scope (labor down) AND/OR raise the rate. May also be a candidate for amicable churn.

What we look for: Operator recognizes that some gaps are too large to fix with a rate increase alone. The math forces the conversation, not the other way around.

Q7 — Common mistake (multiple choice)

Why is it a mistake to lead with the company average RPLH in a customer conversation?

  • a) The average is harder to calculate
  • b) Owners cannot take action on an "average" — they can only act on specific customers
  • c) The average changes too often
  • d) The average is usually wrong

Answer: b

Why it matters: Operators who lead with averages produce no action. The whole point of the conversation is action.

Q8 — Edge case (judgment)

A customer's RPLH is showing as $850/hour. They have $850 in revenue and 1 hour of labor logged. Is this customer beating the target?

Sample strong answer:

No. The customer should be excluded under the low-labor-signal rule (under 10 hours). RPLH is noisy when labor is tiny. The $850 number is mathematically correct but operationally meaningless.

What we look for: Operator understands the exclusion rules and doesn't mistake noise for performance.

Q9 — PioneerOps source knowledge (multiple choice)

In PioneerOps, the labor side of RPLH comes from:

  • a) All pioneer_service_sessions
  • b) Cleaning labor only (excludes inspection, supply-station, hiring, training, management)
  • c) Time tracked in QuickBooks Payroll
  • d) Deputy shift bridge

Answer: b

Why it matters: Inspection and overhead time would penalize customers who happen to get inspected more often. We exclude those. Operators must be able to explain this when a customer asks.

Q10 — Defense under pressure (judgment)

The customer's CEO challenges the math: "These numbers must be wrong. I've been running this business for 20 years and I'd know if some customers were that bad."

What do you say?

Sample strong answer:

"You absolutely know your business. Let's verify the math together right now. We can open QuickBooks and check the revenue for any customer you pick, and we can open PioneerOps and see the actual logged hours. I'd rather we trace one customer line by line than have you take my word for it."

What we look for:

  • Operator does NOT defend the number
  • Operator INVITES verification (transparency = credibility)
  • Operator's stance is collaborative, not adversarial
  • Operator preserves the relationship while preserving the math

Common weak answer: "I'm confident in the numbers, they're pulled directly from your QuickBooks." (Defensive. Misses the chance to teach the owner where the numbers come from.)

ELEMENT 11 — Coaching Discussion

The Friday debrief after this lesson. Trainer asks the operator. No leading questions. Listen for diagnostic thinking, not memorized answers.

  1. Pick one of your owned accounts. What's their current RPLH and where do you think they should be? Walk me through why.
  2. What surprised you in this lesson? Anything that pushed back on how you were thinking about customer profitability before?
  3. The drivers table — which driver have you actually seen in the field, in the last two weeks? On which customer?
  4. Imagine the owner of your hardest-to-have-conversation-with account asks you about RPLH tomorrow. What's the opening sentence you'd say?
  5. What did this lesson NOT prepare you for? What's the next thing you want to practice on this topic?
